## Preface

This textbook introduces quantum mechanics to undergraduate students who are encountering the subject for the first time. The central goal is to connect the mathematics of quantum mechanics — the wave function, the Schrödinger equation, energy quantization — to something students can see and interact with. Each chapter pairs standard derivations with guided simulation exercises that students build themselves using language-model tools. When a student can watch a wave function evolve and check whether it matches an analytic result, the mathematics becomes a description of something observable rather than an abstract formalism.

The approach taken here reflects two developments that are now available to introductory students. First, capable language models can generate working interactive simulations quickly when directed carefully. Second, the verification habits required to use those tools responsibly — comparing numerical output to analytic results, confirming that probability remains normalized, checking limiting cases — are exactly the habits that constitute careful physics reasoning. This book treats simulation and verification as part of learning the physics, not as supplementary material.

The text is written for undergraduates with a working knowledge of single-variable calculus. We assume the student can take a derivative and evaluate a definite integral; we build everything else from those foundations. The mathematical development is complete enough to follow without gaps, and worked examples are included at each major step. We use "we" throughout to signal that derivations are meant to be followed actively, not simply read.

This book does not aim to be encyclopedic. It covers the core one-dimensional theory — the wave function, the Schrödinger equation, the standard potential wells, and the uncertainty principle — in enough depth for students to reason carefully about each topic and build simulations that make the results visible. Topics in three dimensions, spin, and many-body systems are left for subsequent volumes. The book also does not teach programming from the beginning; it teaches students to read, modify, and verify the code their tools produce.
