## Preface

Quantum mechanics was not handed down whole. It was assembled, over a quarter
century, by people who were mostly trying to solve narrower problems and who
were frequently uncomfortable with what they found. Planck wanted to fit a
curve and ended up introducing a constant he spent years trying to explain
away. Born corrected a probability formula in a footnote added in proof and
won a Nobel Prize for the footnote, decades later. De Broglie's thesis nearly
failed. Davisson and Germer found the decisive evidence for matter waves
because a flask exploded in their laboratory and forced them to re-anneal a
nickel target. This book is written on the conviction that you understand the
theory better when you watch it being built — when you see which idea came
first, who got it wrong and why their mistake was reasonable, and what
experiment or argument finally broke the deadlock.

That historical spine carries a second commitment: you should be able to hold
these objects, not just read about them. The wave function has no classical
analogue. You cannot picture it the way you picture a thrown ball, and the
standard textbook gives you no way to turn it over in your hands. So this book
pairs every chapter with a simulation you build yourself — governed by physics
you can derive, responding when you change a slider. The tooling for that is
laid out in Chapter 0; it relies on a large language model as a drafting
partner and on a verification discipline strict enough that the model's
mistakes get caught before they reach you.

The book is written for students meeting quantum mechanics for the first time
at the upper-division level, and for anyone who has met it before and felt that
the formulas arrived without their history. It does not cover everything. It
stops where Volume 1 stops — at the foundations, through the infinite square
well — and it leaves perturbation theory, angular momentum, and the hydrogen
atom for later. What it tries to do, in the space it has, is show you that the
equations are answers to questions real people asked, and to put a working
model of each answer in front of you while you read.
