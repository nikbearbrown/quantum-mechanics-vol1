## Preface

Many of us first meet quantum mechanics as a wall of equations. The mathematics is correct and the predictions are extraordinary, yet the objects the equations describe stay stubbornly out of reach. A wave function is not something we can hold, turn over, or point at. This book exists to close that gap. Alongside the text and the mathematics, we give you a third thing to work with: simulations you build yourself, governed by physics you can derive, that respond when you change a parameter. When the wave function becomes something you can watch and adjust, the equations stop being a barrier and become a description of something you have already seen.

The timing matters. Capable language models can now help you produce working interactive simulations quickly, provided you direct them carefully. We treat that skill as part of learning the physics, not separate from it. Every chapter pairs the standard derivations with a guided exercise in which you build, verify, and reason about a simulation. The verification habits we teach — checking a number against an analytic result, checking that probability stays normalized — are the same habits that make you a careful physicist, with or without a computer.

This book is written for undergraduates meeting quantum mechanics for the first time, including those who arrive without much confidence that they belong here. We assume you can take a derivative and a definite integral, and we build everything else from there. We use "we" throughout because the work is shared: we derive results together, we check them together, and we are honest together about what remains genuinely unsettled at the foundations of the subject.

A word on what this book does not do. It does not aim to be encyclopedic, and it does not teach you to program from scratch. It teaches you to read and modify what your tools produce, and to never trust a result you cannot check. If you finish these chapters able to derive the physics, build a simulation that shows it, and tell the difference between a result that is right and one that merely looks right, the book has done its job.
