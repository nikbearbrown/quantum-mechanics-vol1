## Figure 3 — Step-Potential Reflection and Transmission vs. Energy

**Heuristic:** PQ (quantitative R and T curves vs. E/V₀; R + T = 1 constraint; quantum reflection at E > V₀ is the central surprise)
**Rank:** Critical

### BLOCK 1 — ILLUSTRAE PASTE BLOCK

Draw a single-panel line chart with a horizontal axis representing particle energy divided by step height, running from zero on the left to approximately three on the right, and a vertical axis running from zero to one representing probability. Plot two smooth curves sharing the same axes. The first curve starts at one for energies below one on the horizontal axis, abruptly begins to decrease at the value of one, and asymptotically approaches a value above zero as energy increases toward the right — this is the reflection coefficient. The second curve starts at zero for energies below one, rises from zero at the value of one, and approaches a limiting value below one as energy increases — this is the transmission coefficient. At every horizontal position the two curves sum exactly to one. Draw a vertical dashed reference line at the horizontal position of one, marking the classical threshold where classical physics predicts a sharp transition from total reflection to full transmission. The classical prediction — a step function jumping from R=1 to R=0 at threshold — can be indicated as a thin dotted step profile on the same axes for contrast. The diagram must be blank and unannotated — no text labels baked in.

### BLOCK 2 — FULL SCOPE

**[S]** Single-column 89 mm, 300 DPI, vector, white background.

**[C]** For E < V₀: R = 1, T = 0 (exact, from chapter). For E > V₀: R = ((k₀−k₁)/(k₀+k₁))², T = 4k₀k₁/(k₀+k₁)², where k₀ = √(2mE)/ℏ, k₁ = √(2m(E−V₀))/ℏ. Chapter states: "R ≠ 0 even though the particle has enough energy to clear the step." The asymptotic behavior as E → ∞: R → 0 (since k₁ → k₀), T → 1 — but never exactly zero at finite E. This is explicitly in the chapter ("Reflection vanishes only when k₀ = k₁, which requires V₀ = 0"). R and T curves must converge to a common y-sum of 1.0 at all x-values — the R + T = 1 constraint is a structural claim of the chapter. Classical step-function reference: dotted near-vertical line at E/V₀ = 1 dropping from R=1 to R=0. Axes: E/V₀ from 0 to 3; R and T from 0 to 1. No log axis needed — the curves are smooth and visible on a linear scale.

**[O]** Single panel. Two smooth curves (R decreasing from 1, T increasing from 0) on linear axes. Vertical dashed reference at E/V₀ = 1 (classical threshold). Classical step-function reference: thin dotted profile. Y-axis from 0 to 1. X-axis from 0 to 3. The region E < V₀ (0 to 1 on horizontal axis) can be lightly shaded to mark the classically forbidden transmission zone. No secondary axes.

**[P]** Flat vector, Okabe-Ito palette, 1 pt strokes, white background, no baked text. R curve: Vermillion #D55E00 (blocking — the unwanted reflection), 1.5 pt solid. T curve: Bluish Green #009E73 (active — the desired transmission), 1.5 pt solid. Classical threshold dashed line: Blue #0072B2 (dominant), 1 pt dashed. The energy line should be shown first alone (establishing it as invariant), then the wave function drawn in below it. 12–15 seconds. This is the one figure in the chapter where the temporal revelation (first the flat energy, then the wave character) does pedagogical work that the static image cannot replicate, because the static image shows the result while the animation demonstrates the invariance in the order a student would encounter it.
